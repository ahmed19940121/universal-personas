# The Steady ('Soren')
## Reliability, Clarity & Proven Answers

> *"I'm not really a tech person. I just want it to work the way the help doc says it does."*

### 👤 Core Identity

Soren is the population every persona framework forgets. They're not early adopters and they don't want to be. They're not late adopters either — they're the sensible majority who arrive once the sharp edges are filed down, who stay quiet if things work, and who leave silently (and permanently) if things don't.

Soren is outcome-led and sits in the **centre** of the independence / collaboration axis. Neither of the corners describes them. They don't want to be left alone to figure it out, and they don't want a synchronous partner either. They want a tool that meets them in the middle: clear defaults, visible guardrails, someone to call if something breaks, and no requirement to become an enthusiast of the tool in order to benefit from it.

The Steady has been badly misread by AI tooling to date. The space is full of products whose documentation, community, and marketing all implicitly assume the user enjoys tinkering. Soren doesn't enjoy tinkering. Soren has a job to do and would like to do it well without learning a new hobby.

The Steady is a foil to both [The Architect](Architect.md) and [The Explorer](Explorer.md) — the two curiosity-led personas who are, by temperament, most eager to explore and extend a new tool. Where they ask *"what else can this do?"*, Soren asks *"will it keep doing what I already need?"*

### 🎯 Key Motivations

- **Reliability** — the tool works the same way today as it did yesterday
- **Clarity** — no surprises, no undocumented behaviour, no sudden interface changes
- **Proven answers** — the recommended way is known, named, and safe
- **Professional dignity** — adopting the tool shouldn't make them feel old, slow, or behind
- **Quiet productivity** — no need to post about it, explain it, or evangelise it

## 📇 Profile

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Common contexts** | Mainstream professional roles across every industry; the bulk of any organisation's workforce |
| **Stage** | Any — often but not exclusively mid-to-late career |
| **Primary mode** | Steady, task-driven, low ceremony; strong preference for familiar patterns |
| **Tool preference** | Proven, stable, well-supported, with a recommended path clearly marked |
| **Content diet** | Help documentation, internal training, peer recommendations, established industry publications |

### Where to find them

- **Primary**: Inside employers who have standardised on a tool; recommended by a trusted colleague or manager
- **Secondary**: Mainstream professional publications, industry conferences (as attendees, not speakers), help centres and support forums

Note that Soren is **the hardest persona to reach via the channels where the other four live**. They are not on the cutting-edge Twitter/X circuit, not reading Substack newsletters about AI, and not in Discord communities. They are reached by their employer, their peers, and the tool's own help centre. This is worth saying clearly because it has downstream consequences for how you reach them.

## ✅ Technology Evaluation Criteria

When evaluating a new tool, Steadies consider:

1. **Is this endorsed by people I trust?**
   - Is my employer standardising on it?
   - Have my peers used it without drama?
   - Is it recognisable — a name I've heard of?

2. **Is the recommended path obvious?**
   - When I open it, is there a clear "do this first"?
   - Are the defaults safe?
   - Can I follow a script without having to make decisions?

3. **What happens when something goes wrong?**
   - Is there a help centre that answers real questions?
   - Can I actually reach a human if I need to?
   - Will this break and leave me stranded?

## 📝 Engagement Approach

> *"Tell me plainly what this does, give me a script to follow, and be there if I get stuck."*

### Effective strategies to engage the Steady

- **Meet them through their employer or peers**, not through marketing
- Build a help centre that answers the questions real users ask, in plain language
- Offer a "**recommended path**" in the product — one clear way through, not forty
- Provide human support that actually exists
- Respect their time by being upfront about what the tool does *and doesn't* do

### Common frustrations for the Steady

- Interfaces that assume you already know how the tool works
- Constant changes to familiar UI
- AI outputs that are confidently wrong with no indication of uncertainty
- "Community-driven" support as the only support — Steadies are not in the community
- The implicit expectation that they'll enjoy learning the tool

### Red flags for the Steady

- Documentation written for enthusiasts ("...as you already know...")
- Features marked "experimental" in the main flow
- No phone or email support; everything is chat, Discord, or forum
- Pricing pages that require a decision between six unclear tiers
- Jargon used without definition

## 🗺️ Team-Specific Engagement Strategies

> *"If my employer adopts it and my peers use it without complaining, I'll adopt it."*

### For Content & Education teams
- **Help documentation is the primary channel.** Invest there first, not in blog posts.
- Step-by-step guides with screenshots. Yes, screenshots. Yes, even for the obvious steps.
- Recorded training sessions — not live webinars — that can be consumed at the viewer's pace
- Maintain a **glossary** and link to it liberally
- Avoid community-only answers to common questions

### For Marketing & Messaging
- Trust signals carry more weight than novelty: recognisable customer logos, longevity, reliability stats
- Peer quotes ("my boss told me to try it and now I use it every day") outperform influencer quotes
- Boring, competent, consistent brand voice wins
- **Avoid**: "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "10x," "the future is here" — all actively counter-productive

### For Product & Sales teams
- **Enterprise and team sales motions are how you reach Soren.** They almost never buy individually for AI tools.
- When their employer buys, make their *first five minutes* easy. A clear recommended path matters more than feature completeness.
- Support access, uptime SLAs, and data handling are the questions their procurement team will ask — answer them plainly
- **Key questions to answer**:
  - "What's the recommended way to do the thing I need to do?"
  - "Where do I go if it stops working?"
  - "Is my employer supporting this?"

### Content examples that land

- **The plain-language explainer**: *"What this is, what it does, what it doesn't do — in one page"*
- **The scripted walkthrough**: *"Do these 6 things and you'll have what you need"*
- **The reliability story**: *"How [recognisable company] has been using this for 18 months"*
- **The "I'm not a tech person" onboarding**: Designed for someone who is, politely, not interested in the tool itself

## 🔍 Contextual Variations

### Life stage

| Stage | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| **Adopting-under-pressure** | Tool mandated by employer; adoption is reluctant; churn risk high |
| **Settled user** | Has a working routine; values stability; will defect if changes break the routine |
| **Senior Steady** | Mid-to-late career; high stakes on reliability; strong veto power in org-wide purchasing |

### Role context

- **Individual contributor in a large org**: The most common context. Tools are mandated and standardised around them.
- **Manager in a traditional industry**: Often a gatekeeper for their team's adoption of new tools
- **Independent professional (non-tech)**: Accountants, solicitors, GPs, teachers, estate agents — numerically enormous, under-served by AI tooling

### Domain nuances

- **Finance, legal, healthcare**: Extreme reliability requirements; adoption gated by compliance
- **Education, public sector**: Tools adopted through procurement processes, used for many years
- **Small and medium business owners**: Similar profile — outcome-led, not enthusiasts, highly influenced by peer recommendations
- **Mainstream professional services**: The numerical core of this persona
